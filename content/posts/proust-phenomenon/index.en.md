Has the scent drifting from a bakery ever brought back your grandmother's kitchen — whole and complete — something you'd long forgotten?

## A name born from a single madeleine

The novelist Marcel Proust wrote a scene in which an entire childhood comes flooding back from the aroma of a madeleine dipped in tea. That's why the phenomenon of smell evoking vivid memories is called the 'Proust phenomenon.' It isn't just a literary metaphor — the brain is actually built that way.

## A 'shortcut' only smell knows

The key is a 'shortcut.' What we see and hear passes through a relay station called the thalamus before reaching the brain. But smell is the exception. Scent signals entering through the nose skip the relay station and go straight to the amygdala (emotion) and the hippocampus (memory). It's as if a 'direct line' is wired to the regions in charge of emotion and memory. That's why memories triggered by smell are unusually vivid — and steeped in emotion.

## Early childhood, and marketing

Two intriguing facts. First, smell memories are mostly formed before about age ten, so the scenes that surface are usually from very early childhood. Second, perfume and diffuser marketing targets this very principle. Tie a particular scent to a brand, and later that smell alone can awaken a consumer's emotions.

## A door to your oldest memory

A scent you brushed past without a thought today may, in fact, have been a door leading to your very oldest memory.
