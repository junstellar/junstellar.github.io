There was a time when painters had to be paid extra by their patrons just to use this one shade of blue.

## A color squeezed from a gemstone

In the Renaissance, the most precious color in the world was 'ultramarine blue.' The pigment came from a gemstone called lapis lazuli, and at the time nearly the only source was a single mine deep in the mountains of Afghanistan. Because it had to travel a long way 'across the sea' (ultra marinus) to reach Europe, the name itself came to mean "the color from beyond the sea."

## A pigment costlier than gold

Just how expensive was it? It cost more than its weight in gold. Painters couldn't use it just anywhere — contracts for paintings even included clauses stating that "ultramarine is paid for separately." In the end, this precious blue was reserved for the most sacred subjects, above all the robes of the Virgin Mary. The blue of the headscarf in Vermeer's 'Girl with a Pearl Earring' is this very pigment.

## 1828: a color for everyone

The fate of this blue changed in 1828. When a method for synthesizing artificial ultramarine was developed in France, its astronomical price plummeted overnight. A nobleman's color once squeezed from gemstones became an ordinary paint anyone could use.

## A whole history in a single color

A single color can hold within it the history of mines and trade routes, of faith and science — which is why, the next time you look at a painting, it's worth taking a second look at the colors.
