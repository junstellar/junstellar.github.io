Let's start with a quiz. One company books ₩333 trillion in revenue, the other ₩97 trillion. Who earned more profit? Common sense says the former — yet **2025 operating profit came in at ₩47.2T for SK hynix versus ₩43.6T for Samsung Electronics.** A company one-third the size out-earned the whole of Samsung for the first time in history. How is that possible? The answer lies in **business structure**. [Part 1](/en/p/what-is-hbm/) and [part 2](/en/p/hbm4-war/) covered the HBM weapon; today we compare the physiques of the two companies wielding it.

## Samsung is a 'department store'

We call Samsung a semiconductor company, but strictly speaking it's a **general electronics department store that also sells chips**. More than half of its ₩333.6T revenue in 2025 (₩188T) came from the DX division — smartphones, appliances, TVs. Semiconductors (DS) are one floor below, and that floor subdivides again into memory, foundry (contract manufacturing), and System LSI. Add displays (SDC) and automotive/audio (Harman).

![Business structure comparison of Samsung and SK hynix — department store vs specialty shop](structure-compare.png)

The implication is clear: **no matter how strong memory is, weakness on other floors dilutes the total report card.** Memory boomed in 2025, but foundry losses and softer smartphone margins ate into profits, leaving Samsung's operating margin at around 13%.

## SK hynix is a 'specialty shop'

SK hynix, by contrast, earns over 90% of revenue from memory (DRAM, HBM, NAND) — a **pure-play memory specialist**. No other floors, no dilution. Memory-boom profits flow straight through to the company's bottom line.

The result: an **operating margin of 48.6%**. Selling ₩100 worth of goods and keeping ₩49 — all year long — is almost unheard of in manufacturing. It's what happens when HBM's traits from part 2 (expensive, long-term contracts, ~70% share) meet the specialty-shop structure.

## Why the stocks move differently

That difference in physique shows up directly in the stocks.

| | Samsung | SK hynix |
|---|---|---|
| Character | Diversified electronics giant | Pure-play memory |
| In a memory boom | Gains get diluted | Hits earnings and stock directly |
| In a memory bust | Other businesses cushion | Takes the full blow (losses possible) |
| Stock volatility | Relatively low | High (high beta) |
| Extra variables | Foundry turnaround, smartphones | HBM share and yield |

In short, **hynix is an amplifier of the memory cycle; Samsung is a shock absorber.** The stronger your conviction in the AI memory supercycle, the more attractive hynix's pure exposure — and the bigger its downside when the cycle turns. Samsung can't fully capture memory upside, but it holds **its own re-rating cards**: a foundry turnaround, or the HBM4 counterattack from part 2.

## What investors should take away

- **"I bought Samsung because semiconductors look good"** — there's a trap in that sentence. If you want pure exposure to a memory boom, the structural answer is hynix; buying Samsung means buying a package of memory + foundry + smartphones + a valuation-discount story.
- **Buying hynix** = betting on the memory (especially HBM) cycle continuing. Checkpoints: HBM share and yield (part 2), and where we are in the cycle (part 4).
- **Buying Samsung** = a diversified bet on the cycle plus non-memory recovery plus re-rating. Lower volatility — and the same structure explains why it lags hynix in an up-cycle.

## Recap

- In 2025 hynix's operating profit (₩47.2T) surpassed all of Samsung (₩43.6T) for the first time — **a victory of structure, not size**.
- Samsung is a **department store** where chips are one floor; hynix is a **specialty shop** where memory is everything. They are not the same "semiconductor stock."
- Hynix **amplifies** the cycle (high risk, high reward); Samsung **cushions** it (low volatility plus re-rating cards). What you buy is what you believe.
