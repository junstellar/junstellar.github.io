A few years ago, a "transformer company" was the most boring stock imaginable — a smokestack business with no growth, maybe a dividend. Yet HD Hyundai Electric has multiplied several times over the past two years, past ₩40 trillion in market cap — a run to rival any chip leader. Not a power plant, not a chipmaker — so why did this "transformer maker" become an AI-era protagonist?

[Part 2](/en/p/smr-nuclear-ai/) covered generation (nuclear/SMR). Today is the next chokepoint: **delivery** — the gear that carries plant-made electricity to the data center: transformers, cables, the grid. Up front: there's a real supercycle here. But its character differs subtly from chips, and knowing that difference is today's point.

## Why transformers are the bottleneck

You don't just make electricity at a plant and plug it into a data center. To send it far, you crank the voltage way up (high-voltage transmission), then bring it back down where it's used (distribution) — and the device that raises and lowers that voltage is the **transformer**. Large data centers in particular need dedicated substations and ultra-high-voltage transformers to pull in hundreds-of-thousands-of-households worth of power. No matter how much you generate, without transformers to carry it, the electricity never reaches the data center.

The problem is that three things hit at once.

![The three drivers of the transformer supercycle — aging grid + AI demand + supply shortage](transformer-supercycle.png)

**① The US grid is old.** 70% of US transmission lines are over 25 years old, and large transformers average over 40 years. It was due for replacement anyway. **② Then AI hit.** US data-center power demand is projected to grow from 24GW in 2026 to 110GW by 2030 — up 358%. **③ But supply can't keep up.** Transformer lead times ballooned from a normal ~1 year to as much as 4 years (160+ weeks), and prices are up over 70% since 2020. Only one company in the US makes the key material — grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES) — so lead times structurally can't be cut.

Demand explodes while supply can't grow — part 1's imbalance shows up most extremely in transformers. As a result, Korea's three heavy-electric firms have piled up a combined backlog over ₩32T — four to five years of work — and transformer exports to the US topped ₩1T in a quarter for the first time.

## Why Korea's three firms are winning

Here Korea is strong. Ultra-high-voltage transformers, especially the 765kV class, are high-difficulty products only a few firms worldwide can make — and Korea's three broke into the US market on low defect rates and reliable delivery.

- **HD Hyundai Electric**: high-margin structure centered on UHV transformers, 71% export ratio. Won a 765kV order from Texas's largest utility; 2026 operating margin above 20%. Stock +194% YTD.
- **LS Electric**: Q1 operating profit up 45%, a record quarter; a broad portfolio adding distribution and automation. Stock +108%.
- **Hyosung Heavy Industries**: the only local 765kV plant in the US (Memphis), holding the top share. Stock +68%.

Within the generation–transmission–distribution flow we'll see in part 5, these three hold the key gate of transmission.

## But — three ways this is decisively different from chips

Read this far and it sounds like "the next semiconductors." But there are differences to state honestly. This is the real point.

![The three firms' scorecards, three ways it differs from chips, and the May–June crash](vs-semiconductor.png)

**① You can't cleanly separate AI demand.** Chips label "this is AI revenue" clearly, in the form of HBM. But a large share of transformer demand isn't AI data centers — it's **just replacing an aging grid**. It rises under the "AI theme," yet even the companies don't cleanly break out what % of revenue is purely AI. The "AI stock" label is looser than for chips.

**② The barrier to entry is lower than chips.** Chips have a giant moat in process miniaturization (parts 1, 5). Transformers can be mass-produced by anyone who builds a line to standard. China actually makes 40% of the world's transformers and is eyeing US entry via Mexico and elsewhere. Korea's current edge (fast delivery, low defects) is real skill — but not a chip-like overwhelming moat.

**③ It's a classic cyclical.** The "long lead time" that's the source of today's premium is, flipped over, the risk. The whole world is expanding transformer plants, and the moment lead times normalize, the price premium can deflate. Shipbuilding endured a long slump after its 2000s supercycle amid mass expansion and Chinese catch-up. "Boom → expansion race → oversupply" is the fate of cyclicals (part 4's pork-cycle logic exactly).

So these stocks already got shaken hard once. **In May–June, all three fell 30–37%** (HD Hyundai -31%, Hyosung -37%, LS -35%). Earnings hadn't deteriorated — it was a correction from over-priced expectations plus high rates and profit-taking. Backlogs stayed solid while the stocks swung — the kind of volatility (from part 8, [KOSPI concentration](/en/p/kospi-semiconductor/)) that happens where expectations ran ahead.

## What investors should watch

- **Delivery has more real earnings than generation**: if part 2's SMR is still future revenue, transformers are already proven in backlog and earnings. That's a strength.
- **Watch lead times and expansion**: for the supercycle to hold, lead times (supply shortage) must stay long. When global expansion resolves that is the cycle's peak signal.
- **Doubt the "AI stock" label**: check how much of a firm's revenue is actually AI data centers versus mixed with aging-grid replacement. Part 7's ([suppliers](/en/p/semiconductor-suppliers/)) "actual revenue share, not the theme label" applies here too.

## Recap

- Transformers and cables hold AI's power **delivery** chokepoint. **An aging US grid + surging AI demand + supply shortage (4-year lead times)** created a real supercycle, and Korea's three firms benefited on UHV competitiveness.
- But it differs from chips — **① AI demand can't be cleanly separated (mixed with replacement demand), ② the barrier to entry is low (China makes 40%), ③ it's a cyclical (expansion → oversupply risk).**
- Hence the 30%-range correction already seen in May–June. **Lead-time durability vs expansion glut** is the key thing to watch from here.
