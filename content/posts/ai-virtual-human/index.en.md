## A virtual-human video is made from '3 ingredients'

You can't just tell ChatGPT or Claude "make me a video." A talking virtual human is **three things you build separately and then combine.**

| Ingredient | What it does | Tool I used |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **① Face** | A photo of a person who doesn't exist | SDXL (local image generation) |
| **② Voice** | Turns the script into human speech | edge-tts (free) |
| **③ Lip motion** | Lip-syncs the mouth to the voice | SadTalker (free, local) |

All three are **free and run on my own GPU without internet.** Let's go through them.

## ① Face — sculpting 'Ari' with SDXL

First you need a face that doesn't exist in the world. I generated several "neat, trustworthy Korean female anchor" images locally with SDXL (Stable Diffusion XL) and picked the one I liked. If you **fix the seed**, you can produce multiple shots of the same person while only changing the expression or angle. That's how the fixed character "Ari" was born.

![The face of 'Ari', an AI-generated virtual anchor](cover.png)

## ② Voice — free Korean speech with edge-tts

I kept the script very short: *"Hello, I'm Ari, generated by AI. Nice to meet you."* I turned that line into a Korean audio file with Microsoft's **edge-tts** (a free TTS). The female voice `ko-KR-SunHiNeural` sounds quite natural.

## ③ Lip motion — lip-sync with SadTalker

This is the core. **SadTalker** is an open-source project that takes a single photo and an audio file and produces a video where the mouth and expression move along with the voice. It's completely free and runs on my GPU. Feed it the face photo plus the voice, and you're done.

But this is where the **trial and error** began. It doesn't come out pretty by default. Here are the tips that actually worked.

### Tip 1. The source photo must have a 'closed mouth'

At first I fed it a wide smile showing teeth, and the mouth area smeared into something **alien-like**. It turns out SadTalker produces far more natural lip-sync when you give it a **calm, closed-mouth photo** as the source. With an open-mouth photo, it's forced to paste lip-sync over that already-open mouth, and it breaks.

![An open-mouth source (left) yields awkward lip-sync; a closed-mouth source (right) is far more natural](src-comparison.png)

Replacing the source with a **closed-mouth version** of the same person (fixed seed) improved it dramatically.

### Tip 2. Keep 'expression_scale' modest

Setting the expression-strength option (`expression_scale`) to 1.2 exaggerated the mouth and looked unnatural. Around **1.0** was just right.

### Tip 3. Use the '_full' output for head-and-shoulders

SadTalker produces two output videos. One is a **face-only crop**; the other, with `_full` in its filename, composites the animated face back onto the **entire original photo (shoulders and upper body included)**. If the face feels too cropped-in, use the `_full` one. I wasted a while not knowing this.

### Tip 4. (Install) The cu121 PyTorch combo is the safe bet

The most common trap during setup is the GPU build of PyTorch. On a PC with a recent graphics driver, the older `cu118` (torch 2.0.1) combo can throw a `c10_cuda.dll` load error (WinError 127). Installing **`torch 2.1.2 + torchvision 0.16.2` (cu121)** is stable and compatible with the libraries SadTalker needs.

## How much GPU do you need

Not much. **8GB of VRAM is enough.** I ran it on a laptop RTX 4060 (8GB); an RTX 3070-class card works the same. A short 6-second clip takes a few minutes, and turning on face enhancement (gfpgan) is no burden. That said, lip-syncing an **8-minute video** end to end means tens of thousands of frames and takes quite a while, so for long lectures it's more realistic to place the presenter small in a corner of the screen.

## An honest conclusion — where it works and where it stops

- **Works:** For free, on my own PC, you can make a nonexistent person read your script. That's plenty for personal projects, learning, and prototypes.
- **Limits:** No matter how many tips you apply, the mouth area never quite reaches "perfectly natural." Look closely and you can tell. If you want **broadcast-grade smoothness**, commercial web services (paid) like HeyGen or Synthesia are the answer — and even then you can feed in the SDXL-made face as the avatar.
- **License note:** SadTalker was originally released for research, so if you use it in monetized content, it's safest to double-check the license.

The fact that you can get this far without spending money shows just how much AI tools have lowered the barrier. Next, I plan to attach slides to "Ari" and have her give an actual lecture.
