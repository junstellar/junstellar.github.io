In [part 1](/en/p/what-is-hbm/) we covered what HBM is and why it's called the "rice of the AI era." But one question remains: three companies can make it — SK hynix, Samsung, and Micron — so **why did only SK hynix thrive?** At its peak, a 70% market share. How did memory's perennial runner-up come to dominate this market, and why is that dominance now being shaken? Today, the inside story of this battle.

## Where the 70% number comes from

By market research estimates, SK hynix held roughly 70% of the HBM market in 2025, with Samsung and Micron splitting the rest — a de facto one-company reign. That number is why hynix's stock rose earlier and faster than Samsung's, and it underpins those aggressive price targets. This dominance wasn't luck; it was the compound result of **three ingredients**.

![Diagram of the three keys behind SK hynix's 70% HBM share](hynix-three-keys.png)

## Key ① An alliance built a decade early

As we saw in part 1, SK hynix invented HBM in 2013 — long before the AI boom, when **hardly anyone wanted to buy it**. While Samsung eased off, judging the market too small, hynix quietly kept grinding at this unpopular niche and built its relationship with Nvidia.

Then ChatGPT flipped the world in 2022. Practically the only company able to supply HBM3 for Nvidia's AI chips on time and at quality was SK hynix. In semiconductors, customer qualification takes months to a year — **catching up to a rival who earned trust first is brutally hard.** A decade of early investment came back as near-exclusive supply.

## Key ② MR-MUF, the yield weapon

Part 1's rule: one defective layer scraps the whole HBM stack, so only companies that master yield make money. Hynix's yield weapon is a proprietary packaging process called **MR-MUF** — stack the chips, inject a liquid protective material into the gaps, and cure it in one go. Compared with the film-based method competitors used, it dissipates heat better, runs faster, and yields fewer defects.

Making the same HBM but getting more sellable chips out of every batch — that was the direct source of the earnings gap.

## Key ③ The 'safe driving' strategy and the triple alliance

Hynix's choices for next-generation HBM4 are also telling. For the DRAM layers it reuses a fully proven process (1b) to minimize production risk, and for the bottom **base die it partnered with TSMC**, the world's top foundry. From HBM4 onward, customers increasingly want custom features built into that base die — so hynix handed it to the master of logic processes. The result is what's called the Nvidia–TSMC–hynix "AI triple alliance."

## But the board is shaking — Samsung strikes back

That was the story of the reign; here's the part that matters more to investors now. **Early this year, Samsung became the world's first to mass-produce HBM4 and broke into Nvidia's supply chain**, with AMD shipments following. The specs are aggressive too: 11.7Gbps — well above the required 10Gbps — built on a one-generation-ahead DRAM process (1c) and a 4nm base die. The exact opposite of hynix's proven-process playbook — a head-on charge with the newest technology — and this time it worked.

![HBM market share shift — from one-company reign to a three-way race](hbm-share-shift.png)

Market forecasts have shifted accordingly: 2026 HBM share is projected at roughly hynix 50%, Samsung 28%, Micron 22% — a **three-way race**. One thing to keep in mind, though: demand for HBM4 on Nvidia's next platform "Rubin" is so large that **no single supplier can cover it all**. The competition is intensifying, but this is not a game where someone's pie disappears.

## What investors should watch

- **SK hynix**: the dominance premium is priced in, so the pace of share erosion is the question. That said, 50% is still a commanding lead — whether its yield (= profitability) edge holds is the key checkpoint.
- **Samsung**: HBM weakness was a discount factor on the stock, so if the HBM4 counterattack converts into real volume and earnings, there's room for re-rating.
- **The market**: more competition ≠ smaller market. Rubin-driven demand is growing the pie itself, so all three suppliers can grow revenue while share merely gets redistributed.

## Recap

- Hynix's 70% was the compound result of **a decade-early Nvidia alliance + MR-MUF yield + a risk-minimizing strategy**.
- This year Samsung counterattacked as the **world's first to mass-produce and ship HBM4**, reshaping the market from a reign into a three-way race.
- Rubin demand is growing the entire pie — watch the pace of share shifts and each company's yield from here.
