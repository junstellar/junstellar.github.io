## First, last month's mystery

On July 7, Samsung posted preliminary Q2 results: operating profit of **₩89.4 trillion.** Up 1,810% year over year, beating even Nvidia's quarterly profit — effectively the world's best. And that day, Samsung's stock fell nearly **7%.**

Record earnings, and the stock crashes — the most baffling scene for a new investor. Why doesn't "good results, stock up" hold? Solving that mystery is the whole of today's lesson. The answer is one word: **consensus.**

## STEP 1 — not the absolute number, but "vs consensus"

A stock reacts not to whether results were good or bad, but to whether they were **better or worse than expected.** That expectation is the consensus — the average of analysts' estimates. Beat it big and it's an "earnings surprise"; miss it and it's an "earnings shock."

![The five-step order for reading earnings — from vs-consensus to guidance](earnings-reading-order.png)

Samsung's ₩89.4T beat the official consensus (~₩84T), but fell short of what the market actually expected (₩90–100T). And with chips already up so much, the good result was priced in (this is "sell-on," the exhaustion of a catalyst). The release became a trigger for profit-taking instead. So your first calculation on any earnings day is **"operating profit ÷ consensus − 1."** Before you marvel at the absolute figure, run that one line.

## STEP 2–4 — margin, mix, inventory

Look inside the numbers, too.

- **Margin trend**: operating margin tells you direction. Hynix's Q1 margin was 72% — an unreal number for manufacturing. Whether it holds in Q2 is the thing to watch.
- **Quality of the mix**: even when revenue rises, *why* matters. In Q1, hynix's NAND shipments fell 10%, yet profit grew because ASP rose over 70%. As part 5 showed, the pace at which high-value products (HBM, eSSD) grow their share is the real strength.
- **Inventory and capex**: inventory is normally 6–8 weeks; right now it's a hyper-tight ~2. But as part 4 taught, the same inventory reading means different things by phase — low inventory coming out of a glut is a health signal. For capex, watch not the size but whether it's "spending matched to confirmed (pre-ordered) demand."

## STEP 5 — the stock is really moved by guidance

And the most important, last step. The market reacts less to the quarter that just passed than to the **outlook for next quarter (guidance)** — the signals management drops on the call about HBM volume, next year's prices, mass-production roadmaps.

There's a perfect contrast. On June 25, Micron beat consensus on revenue and EPS by 16% and 23%, **and raised next-quarter guidance sharply** — the stock jumped 16%. The opposite of Samsung. Good numbers *and* a signal that it stays good — only then does the stock applaud.

## So on July 29, read hynix like this

In 11 days, the headlines will almost certainly be an absolute number: "hynix operating profit tops ₩70T!" But you now know to look elsewhere.

- **Vs consensus**: the street's Q2 operating-profit consensus is around ₩65T. Yet Korea Investment & Securities and others see ~₩60T — an 8% miss — for a fascinating reason: because HBM runs on long-term contracts (part 1), its prices are fixed in advance, so when commodity DRAM spikes, hynix's ASP growth can actually lag the market average. The target-price spread from part 9 (₩4.3M vs ₩1.85M) comes straight from this estimate gap.
- **Guidance**: is HBM4 mass production slipping to Q3? How does management frame next year's volume and prices? On the last call, hynix said "customer-requested demand over the next three years already far exceeds our supply capacity." Whether that tone holds is the real-world answer to part 4's cycle debate and the extra installment's "money river" question.
- **Don't panic if the stock falls**: like Samsung, a record result can still sink the stock. That's normal, not broken.

## The mistakes investors make most

To wrap up, the landmines commonly stepped on at earnings time.

1. **Ignoring consensus** — judging "good results" from the absolute profit alone.
2. **Dismissing guidance** — drunk on this quarter, missing next quarter's outlook.
3. **Missing the sell-on** — mistaking a priced-in catalyst for a buy signal and entering late.
4. **Overlooking mix** — seeing only the total, missing qualitative shifts like ASP or HBM share.
5. **Skipping the call** — reading only the press-release numbers, missing the signal in what management stresses and avoids.
