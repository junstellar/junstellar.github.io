One line kept coming up as we closed the 10-part [AI Chip Investing 101](/en/p/what-is-hbm/): "AI's real bottleneck is no longer chips — it's **power**." Part 8 noted Meta bolting its own gas plant onto a data center; the extra installment said the problem isn't "built but empty" but "can't build for lack of power." This is where we cash in that setup. If chips were "AI's brain," the star of this new series is **the electricity that feeds it.**

Part 1 defines the problem: what exactly is the data-center power crunch, and why did nuclear, cable, and transformer stocks become as hot as chips? Let's build intuition with numbers.

## The true cost of one question

Start small. You throw one question at ChatGPT. How much electricity does that single query use?

![The power and cooling water of one query — search vs AI](query-cost.png)

A ChatGPT query uses about 2 Wh (watt-hours); a Google search about 0.3 Wh — roughly **7x** more. People often say "10x," but that's an exaggeration; recent data puts it near 7x. One query is a speck. The problem is that these pile up **billions per day**, and as AI replaces search, those 7x-heavier queries are exploding.

And it's not just electricity. GPUs run hot. To cool that heat, one large data center uses **3–5 million gallons of water a day** (5–8 Olympic pools). Running the cooling towers takes electricity too. So a data center's power draw is **electricity for compute + electricity for cooling** — and both are surging.

## So how much does it eat — a whole country's worth

Small queries add up to a terrifying scale.

![Data-center power consumption surge — from 447 TWh in 2025 to 1,200 TWh in 2030](power-surge.png)

Per market researcher Gartner, global data-center electricity use jumps from 447 TWh in 2025 to 565 TWh in 2026 — **+26% in a single year**. AI servers make up 31% of that, and AI-server power alone rises 84% year over year. The IEA projects data-center and AI-related electricity will exceed **1,000 TWh in 2026 — double 2022's level**.

Can't picture 1,000 TWh? It's roughly **all the electricity South Korea uses in a year**. Think of it as a new "virtual nation" of AI data centers appearing on Earth and starting to draw a Korea's worth of power. Even domestically, forecasts say data-center demand will, within three years, reach the level of dedicating an entire nuclear reactor just to run them.

## Why this became an investing theme

Here the chip series' logic carries straight over. Remember the "AI money river" from part 6 — Big Tech capex → Nvidia → hynix? **That river had one more tributary.** Building a data center means buying not only GPUs but **hauling in the electricity** to run them: power plants, transmission lines, transformers, cooling gear — all newly required.

The catch is that electricity doesn't scale as fast as chips. A GPU is stamped out in months; a power plant takes years, a nuclear plant a decade. Even a transmission line takes years of permitting. **Demand (AI) explodes while supply (power) plods** — that imbalance is exactly where investment opportunity is born. Part 4's "pork cycle" logic for chips applies identically to power.

So money flows in three directions:

- **Generation**: you need power supplied reliably around the clock at scale → nuclear (especially SMRs) back in the spotlight. (Part 2)
- **Delivery**: gear to carry electricity to the data center → transformers, cables, the grid. This is why heavy-electric names like HD Hyundai Electric and Hyosung rose as much as chips. (Part 3)
- **Cooling**: the new heat bottleneck → immersion cooling and more. (Part 8)

## Recap

- AI runs on electricity. One ChatGPT query uses **~7x** a Google search's power, and one large data center uses **millions of gallons of cooling water a day**. The cost isn't just the chip.
- The scale is nation-sized — data-center and AI power tops **1,000 TWh in 2026 (a Korea's worth)** and surges further by 2030. AI-server power alone rose 84% in a year.
- The essence of the theme is the imbalance: **AI demand explodes, power supply plods.** Money flows three ways — generation (nuclear/SMR), delivery (transformers/cables), and cooling.

This series follows those three branches one by one. Part 2 tackles the hottest — generation: **why nuclear, and SMRs in particular, suddenly became AI plays.**
