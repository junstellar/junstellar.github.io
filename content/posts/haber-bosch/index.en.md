Half of the nitrogen in your body came from a German chemist's laboratory a hundred years ago.

## Your food actually comes from 'air'

We eat every day, yet we rarely realize that our food came from "air." To grow, plants need nitrogen. But even though 78% of the air is nitrogen, plants can't use it directly. Atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) has its two atoms bound so tightly that, before any living thing can use it, it must first be 'split' and turned into ammonia. For thousands of years, humankind left this job to legumes and animal dung.

## 1909: pulling fertilizer out of the air

In 1909, the German chemist Fritz Haber succeeded in combining nitrogen and hydrogen from the air under high heat and pressure to make ammonia. Soon the engineer Carl Bosch scaled it up to factory size. It was, quite literally, a technology for 'extracting fertilizer from air.' Today, roughly half of the world's population is fed thanks to fertilizer made by this process. About half of the nitrogen that makes up your body right now also began in that laboratory.

## Light and shadow from the same hands

But every light casts a shadow. During World War I, Haber led the development of poison gas, earning the name 'father of chemical warfare.' The hands that saved humanity from starvation and the hands that filled the battlefield with death belonged to the same man.

## Science is only as good as the one who wields it

A single invention saved billions and killed tens of thousands. The power of science is always only as good as the choices of the one who holds it.
