In [part 6](/en/p/ai-money-flow/) we mapped the AI money river: us → Big Tech → Nvidia → Korean memory. But writing it left the most important question hanging: **"will this river actually keep flowing?"** SK hynix and Samsung both sit downstream — if the upstream dries up, everyone dries up together. This extra installment inspects the upper river: the headwaters (OpenAI and Anthropic), the reservoir (Big Tech capex), and a new variable (Meta's rumored cloud entry).

## The upstream volume — still rising, for now

Major hyperscalers plan $600–700B+ of AI infrastructure investment in 2026 and roughly **$2.1 trillion cumulatively over 2026–28.** JP Morgan actually raised its capex growth forecast for this year (52%→63%) and sees dollar amounts growing again in 2027.

One decisive difference from the dot-com bubble: **the money's source is still healthy.** Big Tech's capex-to-free-cash-flow ratio remains below 1 — they're largely building with earnings, not debt. The fiber of 2000 was laid with borrowed money, and collapsed with it.

## Headwaters check ① — OpenAI and Anthropic, moving in opposite directions

At the very top of the river, the model companies' finances show a striking contrast.

![Comparison table of OpenAI's and Anthropic's 2026 financials](two-sources.png)

**OpenAI is the river's biggest risk factor.** Reports point to a ~$25B revenue pace against a projected ~$14B net loss in 2026, with cumulative cash burn in the hundred-billion range through 2029. Heavier still are the infrastructure commitments: after signing compute deals with a nameplate value of $1.4 trillion, OpenAI reportedly trimmed its target to around $600B through 2030. The structure only works **if revenue keeps doubling on schedule** — analysts note that even a 10% miss, compounded across multi-year contracts, raises solvency questions by 2028–29. Whether OpenAI-driven commitment cuts propagate through Oracle and Stargate into semiconductor orders is the key mid-river watch item.

**Anthropic, meanwhile, is becoming the counterexample to "AI doesn't make money."** Reports put its annualized revenue past the $30B mark, with its **first profitable quarter** (operating profit ~$559M) projected for Q2 2026. The secret is customer mix — roughly 80% of revenue is enterprise, and a single developer coding tool generates $2.5B annualized. It's not a business subsidizing free consumer usage; it's businesses paying for work.

The lesson for investors: **having more than one headwater is this river's safety valve.** Track separately whether one source (OpenAI) is drying and whether the other (enterprise AI demand) keeps swelling.

## Aren't data centers saturated?

Bottom line: **the opposite of saturated.** North American data-center vacancy sits at record lows, and new builds are pre-leased years before completion. Today's bottleneck isn't "built but empty" — it's **"can't build for lack of power and land."** Meta raised its capex guidance to $125–145B citing exactly that: competition for land, power, and construction labor. Its 1GW Ohio campus is being built with its own gas power plant because the grid can't wait.

Not saturation, but **indigestion risks are real**: mounting depreciation on GPU assets that last only 4–6 years, the BIS formally warning that "a disappointment in returns could turn the capex boom into a protracted investment bust," and circular-deal concerns in the Nvidia→OpenAI→Oracle loop.

## The new variable — Meta's rumored cloud entry

A fascinating recent report: **Meta is considering renting out its AI compute — "Meta Compute."** Meta is building the most aggressive clusters among hyperscalers — the 1GW "Prometheus" (online 2026) and "Hyperion," scalable to 5GW — and the idea is to lease them out AWS-style rather than use them alone.

The ripples, branch by branch:

- **Positive for the river's longevity**: Meta's capex previously monetized only through ads; a rental business makes the capex itself generate revenue — a stronger mandate to keep building.
- **Competitive negative for the big-three clouds**: more supply pressures compute rental prices. Good news for AI users.
- **Neutral-to-positive for semiconductors**: whether for internal use or rental, HBM and memory still get bought. AI stocks wobbled on the news as a possible "early oversupply signal," but vacancy and power data still point to shortage, not glut.

## The dashboard — five gauges are enough

![Dashboard of sustaining arguments, crack candidates, and five watch indicators](river-dashboard.png)

① **Big Tech capex guidance** — the first signal is the moment upgrades stop. ② **OpenAI's next funding round** — closed or not, and on what terms. ③ **Compute rental prices** — how fast they fall after Meta's entry. ④ **Debt share of capex funding** — earnings-funded turning debt-funded. ⑤ **Power-bottleneck relief news** — the day the bottleneck clears is the day the supply step lands ([part 4](/en/p/semiconductor-supercycle/)'s logic exactly).

## Recap

- The river is swelling, not drying; the bottleneck is **supply (power, land)**, not demand saturation — and it's still funded by cash flow, not debt.
- The headwaters diverge: **OpenAI (-$14B a year, trimmed commitments) is the top risk; Anthropic (first profitable quarter, 80% enterprise) is the proof of monetization.** Track them separately.
- Meta's cloud entry adds a monetization channel for capex — a plus for the river's longevity, neutral-to-positive for chip demand.
- The real test is **2027 onward** — when new fabs and power come online (the supply step) just as OpenAI's required revenue doubling gets tested.
