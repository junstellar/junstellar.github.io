A week after our [first camping trip](/en/p/first-camping-malgeumteo/), our family was already off on the second one. This time it was the auto site at **Gwanggyo Lake Park Family Campground** inside Gwanggyo Lake Park in Suwon — another Friday-to-Saturday overnight right after work.

By trip number two, the whole family was hooked. Strangely enough, once you start pitching a tent, you forget all the stress from work and focus only on the tent. And the best part: you get to talk with your family, comfortably and without distractions.

The reason we picked this campground was simple: my husband's office is in Gwanggyo, so there's no better spot for after-work camping. We booked Friday-to-Saturday... but as luck would have it, a business trip landed on his schedule for Wednesday through Friday that very week.

The original plan went like this: my husband finishes work, heads over early, and pitches the tent; we arrive later at our leisure, eat at our leisure, and enjoy the evening at our leisure. Instead, he came straight back from his business trip, we hastily packed, and everyone arrived at the campground exhausted.

Still, once the tent was up and we had good food in front of us, all the fatigue melted away. That's why we go camping, right?

![Our campsite at night with the Gwanggyo tower lights in the background](camp-night.jpg)

This is the charm of Gwanggyo Lake Park Campground: the lights of Gwanggyo's high-rises glowing behind your tent — camping in the middle of the city, literally. It has a completely different vibe from campgrounds out in nature.

But after dinner, as we settled into the tent, a light rain began to fall. A rain camp on only our second camping trip.

With the rain keeping us busy, I couldn't take many photos of the facilities, but the cooking area was stocked with a refrigerator, a microwave, and even a water purifier — no inconveniences at all. With a shared fridge available, you worry less about your cooler, too.

![The shared refrigerators in the cooking area](fridge.jpg)

![The cooking area with water purifiers, microwaves, and sinks](cooking-area.jpg)

Only my husband used the shower room. We'd heard it was a bit cramped, but when we visited it had just been remodeled — the campground manager proudly told us it was clean with great water pressure and that we absolutely should use it.

The next morning, the rainy teardown began. It was our first rain camp, so we had no rain gear — we got soaked stuffing the wet tent into the car before finally making it home.

And it turns out the real homework of rain camping starts after you get home. A wet tent grows mold if you leave it, so it has to be dried — but where do you dry a tent when you live in an apartment? In the end, we quietly(?) dried it on an empty lot by my husband's office on Sunday. That wasn't easy either.

![The tent spread out on an empty lot to dry](tent-drying-ground.jpg)

![The tent draped over a fence to dry](tent-drying.jpg)

Two lessons from our first rain camp: always pack rain gear regardless of the forecast, and figure out where you'll dry a wet tent *before* you tear down.

Even with the rain, it was a joy — and being in the city, the fact that we can go anytime is this campground's biggest strength. Here's hoping for no rain on the next trip.

**Gwanggyo Lake Park Family Campground** — 57 Gwanggyohosu-ro, Yeongtong-gu, Suwon-si, Gyeonggi-do
