The first page of any economics textbook shows a demand curve sloping down to the right: as prices rise, demand falls. Yet for certain goods, this law flips upside down.

## The Thing That Won't Sell When You Cut the Price

In 1890s America, economist Thorstein Veblen observed the spending of the newly rich and noticed something strange. Some goods sold worse when the price dropped, and flew off the shelves only when it rose. People weren't buying the object — they were buying the status of "someone who can afford this." He called it *conspicuous consumption*. The price tag was a receipt proving one's position, and the higher it went, the stronger its effect.

## The Line Outside the Chanel Store

A century later, the paradox is still alive. It's no accident that lines grow outside Chanel stores every time prices go up. In 2021 the Classic Flap bag nearly doubled in price over five years — yet sales rose with it. Customers didn't say "too expensive to buy," they said "expensive, so I want it more." Goods whose demand rises with price are called *Veblen goods*.

## Price Fools Even the Brain

This isn't just vanity for others' eyes. In a 2008 Stanford experiment, participants rated the very same wine as "much better" when it carried a higher price tag. Brain scans showed the pleasure centers actually lighting up more. Price acted as a *quality signal*, and expectation rewrote the experience itself.

## What Are We Really Buying?

Part of what we pay for an object may in fact be paid to the "self" that object announces to the world. Next time you stand before something that appeals *because* it's expensive, ask yourself: am I buying this thing, or the version of me that can afford it?
