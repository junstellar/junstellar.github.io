That ChatGPT subscription you pay every month — have you ever traced where the money goes? Today we follow its journey. Spoiler: part of it winds its way to **an SK hynix fab in Icheon, Korea.** The pieces from parts 1–5 (HBM, the three-way race, business structures, the cycle, the industry map) connect into a single map today. Once you see how this river flows, the vague phrase "AI stocks" resolves into something sharper: **who receives the water, and in what order.**

## The headwaters — us, then Big Tech

The river starts with us: ChatGPT subscriptions, corporate cloud bills, the ad dollars attached to every search and purchase. Microsoft, Amazon, Google, and Meta collect that money and pour it into capex: "AI demand is exploding — build more data centers."

The scale is staggering. **In 2026 alone, major hyperscalers are investing roughly $700 billion in AI infrastructure** — nearly double last year. Amazon alone around $200B, Alphabet about $180B. Part 4 warned that the cycle turns if AI infrastructure spending cracks — which is why **quarterly capex announcements are the trailer for this entire river's water level.**

![Flow map of the AI money river — from consumers through Big Tech, Nvidia, memory, and suppliers](money-river.png)

## The first floodgate — Nvidia

Data centers need AI servers, and the heart of those servers — the GPU — is a de facto Nvidia monopoly. So the biggest stream of capex flows to Nvidia first: its data-center revenue hit $39.1B in a single quarter, up 73% year over year.

Here part 5's map kicks in. Nvidia is **fabless — it only designs.** To turn designs into chips, the money must split two ways.

## The second floodgate — TSMC and Korean memory

- **TSMC** manufactures the GPU itself. The fruit of its 70% foundry dominance (part 5): a large share of Nvidia's stream flows to Taiwan.
- **SK hynix, Samsung, Micron** supply the HBM bolted beside every GPU (part 1), plus the server DRAM and eSSDs (part 5). **Every AI server sold automatically sells memory with it** — the "rice of the AI era" from part 1 is exactly this junction.

And here's today's core point: the width of this stream is changing.

![Where each data-center dollar goes — chips from 40% to 60%, memory from 2% to 18%](dollar-breakdown.png)

**Memory's share of data-center spending has gone from 2% in 2021 to 18% in 2026 — ninefold.** Memory used to be a rounding error in server costs; with HBM and eSSD getting pricier and more plentiful per server, it became a core axis of data-center cost. The same capex dollar now drops a much bigger share on Korean companies — this structural shift is what let hynix's operating profit overtake all of Samsung (part 3).

## The third floodgate — equipment, materials, power, cooling

The river doesn't end there. For hynix to expand HBM output it must buy equipment (packaging tools like TC bonders), source materials and components, and procure substrates. Data centers themselves devour power gear and cooling. That **third stream — the supplier ecosystem — gets its own stock-level map in part 7.**

## What investors should take away

- **The trickle-down has an order**: Big Tech capex → Nvidia earnings → memory contracts → supplier orders. Upstream signals preview downstream earnings quarters in advance — watch upstream indicators while holding downstream names.
- **Doubting the headwaters is risk management**: everything assumes Big Tech keeps spending. If AI monetization disappoints and capex bends, the whole river shrinks — the "this time is different" debate from part 4.
- **Changing shares move stocks**: like memory's 2%→18%, it's not just the river's total volume but **the changing split at each floodgate** that justifies stock selection.

## Recap

- The AI money river: **us (subscriptions) → Big Tech ($700B capex) → Nvidia (GPUs) → TSMC + Korean memory (HBM) → equipment, power, cooling.**
- Memory's share of the data-center dollar grew **from 2% to 18% in five years** — memory's promotion from component to protagonist is this cycle's structural signature.
- Trickle-down has an order: **predict downstream (memory, suppliers) with upstream indicators (capex, Nvidia earnings)** — and always watch whether the headwaters (AI monetization) are drying up.
