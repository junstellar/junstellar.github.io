In 2024, Microsoft signed a deal that stunned the world: it would **revive the Three Mile Island reactor** — site of the worst US nuclear accident, in 1979 — to power AI data centers, buying 20 years of electricity outright. A dead reactor resurrected by AI: that scene is the symbol of today's "nuclear renaissance."

In [part 1](/en/p/ai-power-hunger/), we said AI's power-crunch money flows three ways — generation, delivery, cooling. Today we take the hottest, **generation** — nuclear, and specifically **SMRs (Small Modular Reactors)** — and ask why they suddenly became AI plays. Up front, though: this theme carries both allure and a trap. I'll be honest about both.

## Why nuclear — the electricity AI actually wants

The electricity an AI data center wants has demanding conditions: **around the clock, at scale, and ideally carbon-free.** Solar and wind cut out when the sun sets and the wind stills (intermittency), clashing with a data center's 24/7 operation. Nuclear meets the condition. That's why an industry once treated as in decline came roaring back thanks to AI.

Enter SMRs. Here's how they differ from conventional large reactors.

![Large reactor vs SMR comparison and Big Tech power-procurement deals](why-smr.png)

A large reactor is high-capacity (1,000MW+) but built entirely on site over **more than a decade**, needing vast land. An SMR is a small reactor (under 300MW) **stamped out as modules in a factory** and assembled on site — 3–5 years to build, and deployable in a distributed way right next to a data center. Think standardized reactors added like Lego blocks, as many as you need. Faster, relatively cheaper, more flexible — so the barrier fell to where Big Tech can contract directly.

And Big Tech already moved: Microsoft's Three Mile Island (835MW), Google–Kairos SMR (500MW), Meta's 6.6GW across three nuclear operators, Amazon–Oklo's 1.2GW SMR deal, OpenAI–Oracle pursuing 4.5GW. The AI-era race for electricity has spilled into nuclear.

## Korea plays the 'nuclear foundry'

In the chip series we called TSMC a foundry that builds chips from others' blueprints (part 5). The same structure appeared in nuclear. US SMR design houses (NuScale, X-energy, TerraPower) do the designs — but only a handful of places worldwide can **actually forge** a reactor's core parts (large castings/forgings like pressure vessels and steam generators). One of them is Doosan Enerbility. Its pitch as a "global nuclear foundry," supplying parts to all three major US SMR developers, is why it emerged as the SMR bellwether. It's also building an ₩800B SMR-dedicated plant in Changwon (20 units/year capacity).

Policy joined in too: the government earmarked ₩900B for nuclear-industry support in 2026 (including ₩300B for SMR manufacturing tech), with a "K-nuclear one-team" lineup where KHNP, KEPCO E&C, and Uri Technology each take design and instrumentation roles.

## But — the 'time lag' between hype and earnings

That was the bull case. Now we must look honestly at the other side. This is today's real point.

![Doosan's SMR revenue estimate vs its stock — announcements are SMR, earnings are gas turbines](narrative-vs-reality.png)

Doosan Enerbility's stock ran from ₩18,000 at the start of 2026 to a peak of ₩139,000 — **3–4x** — with a P/E over 100 (2–3x the sector average). Yet Doosan's SMR revenue in 2026 is only around **₩200–300B** — 1–2% of total revenue of ₩17T. What's actually driving earnings now isn't SMRs but **gas turbines** (it won ~₩1T worth — seven gas turbines — from Elon Musk's xAI). SMR revenue topping ₩3T is a **2030 projection**, nothing more.

So today's price prices in future revenue that hasn't arrived. Part 9's lesson ([Anatomy of a Price Target](/en/p/target-price-anatomy/)) applies exactly — before marveling at a number, look at the assumptions behind it. There's a wide gap between the "SMR bellwether" narrative and current earnings.

The lag is more fundamental:

- **Only two SMRs are in commercial operation worldwide** (a Russian floating unit and a Chinese high-temperature gas reactor). No US- or Korea-designed SMR is running commercially **yet.** Korea's i-SMR targets mid-2030s commercialization — and even that has kept slipping.
- **Big Tech deals differ between "announced" and "actually running."** Google–Kairos delivers power from 2030; Meta's volume comes online across 2027–2034. The timing doesn't match the AI power crunch happening right now.
- Leading US SMR firm NuScale saw its 2023 Utah project canceled after generation costs jumped 53%, and SMR stocks tumbled en masse in February 2026 (Oklo -21%, NuScale -20%).

So it's **gas power** that's actually putting out the immediate fire — fast to build and cheap to run, the most realistic option while the grid can't keep up. It's no coincidence gas turbines are driving Doosan's earnings.

## What investors should watch

- **SMR is a long-term theme; current earnings are gas**: don't blend the two. Today's improvement in nuclear names comes mostly from large reactors and gas turbines; SMR is closer to backlog and future estimates.
- **Watch the conversion speed**: how fast do those flashy Big Tech deals turn into actual operation (mostly post-2030), and backlog into actual revenue? Whether that speed justifies the valuation is the crux.
- **The valuation heat is real**: 3–4x year-to-date and a P/E over 100 already price in a lot of hope. Part 4's ([supercycle](/en/p/semiconductor-supercycle/)) "this time is different" is playing out in a nuclear version right now.

## Recap

- Because AI wants 24/7, large-scale, carbon-free power, nuclear revived — and the **factory-built SMR** emerged as the option you can bolt next to a data center. Big Tech is already signing large procurement deals.
- Korea set up its exposure via a **"nuclear foundry"** (Doosan supplying parts to all three major US SMR firms) plus policy support.
- But there's a wide **lag between hype and earnings** — only two SMRs run commercially worldwide, Doosan's SMR revenue is 1–2% of the total, and most Big Tech power arrives after 2030. Gas turbines, not SMRs, drive today's earnings. **Watch conversion speed, not the narrative.**
