In my [last post](/en/p/camping-start/), I shared how we decided to start camping. Today is the story of that very first trip. In April 2023, my two daughters (11 and 9) and I headed to **Malgeumteo Park Campground** in Osan, Gyeonggi-do, for a Friday-to-Saturday overnight stay.

Even after deciding "for this money, let's just buy a tent," honestly, I still had so many worries. We only had a sedan, the kids were young, it seemed exhausting, it seemed expensive... I kept hesitating with one excuse after another.

Then, while I was still agonizing over it, the kids said they wanted to go camping — and at some point I noticed the whole family watching camping YouTube videos. Seeing that, I thought, "Let's start before it's too late!" and just went for it.

Once we decided to go camping, the first thing I did was figure out "so what do we actually need to buy?" After digging through YouTube and blogs for a good while, the must-haves for starting out boiled down to five things:

1. **Tent**
2. **Groundwork** (mats & bedding)
3. **Table & chairs**
4. **Lantern**
5. **Stove & cookware**

The list itself is simple — the problem is deciding *which one* of each to buy. Our situation: sedan campers, a whole family of beginners, four people. So we set three buying criteria: **① everything must pack small, ② everything must be easy enough for beginners, and ③ don't buy anything that something at home can replace.**

**Tent** — The biggest expense, so we applied the criteria most strictly here: light, compact, and easy to pitch. Filtering by those three cut the endless options down fast, and after seeing display models at a store we settled on the Kovea Ghost Phantom. I strongly recommend seeing a pitched tent in person rather than buying from photos — colors especially look completely different in real life.

**Groundwork** — The more I researched, the more I kept reading that "half of camping satisfaction is your sleeping setup," so we didn't skimp here. If you can't block the cold from the ground, camping becomes an ordeal. We went with a foam mat plus a self-inflating mat, and an electric heating pad for spring and fall. Sure enough, the only reason the kids slept warm on an April night was that heating pad. The catch: mats can't be compressed, making this the one category where we had to give up on criterion ①.

**Table & chairs** — We chose by **packed volume**, not unfolded size. Whether it fits in a sedan trunk was our absolute standard.

**Lantern** — Being beginners, we bought just one bright one. That turned out to be the right call, though the campground got far darker after sunset than we expected. I didn't anticipate not being able to see whether the meat was cooked.

**Stove & cookware** — This is where we applied criterion ③ most diligently. No camping cooksets — we just packed our kitchen frying pan, a pot, and a portable gas stove. The only addition was a fire pit, for the wood-fire barbecue dream.

Aesthetic gear like string lights and tarps tempted us too, but since they're not essentials, we held off. After a few trips we'll naturally see what our family actually needs. We decided to trust the veteran campers who say buying everything upfront just means more luggage and unused gear.

Even buying that sparingly, the biggest worry of sedan camping was still the luggage. We had picked the most compact options possible, but things like electric mats and foam mats simply can't be compressed — and the self-inflating mat didn't help either. With a family of four, there was no way to travel light. Thankfully, everything fit.

![Camping gear laid out in the parking lot before loading into the sedan](packing.jpg)

At Malgeumteo Park Campground, you call the campground from the parking lot entrance to open the barrier and request a cart, which then carries your luggage to your site. The cart was smaller than expected, though — we ended up stacking bags at our feet and holding some in our laps, riding along nervously like a game of Tetris where one wrong move could send everything tumbling.

![A cart fully loaded with camping gear passing under the park's footbridge](cart.jpg)

Our site was Kkasaizone 18. Since we left right after work on Friday, we arrived around 6 PM. The goal: pitch the tent before sunset!

Remember how one of the reasons we chose the Ghost Phantom was that it's "easy enough for beginners to pitch"? Well, we were beginners. We didn't even know where to drive the stakes. I had studiously watched YouTube videos saying "drive the stakes so the Kovea logo faces the back" — but I looked at the wrong Kovea logo... After driving all the stakes, we realized the tent was backwards, so we pulled them all out and started over. And why did I hammer them so deep and firm the first time? It took about an hour to pitch the tent, and by the time we organized the inside, the sun had already set.

I originally wanted the khaki color, but when the kids saw the display models at the Kovea Dongtan store, they preferred tan. So tan it was — and honestly, once pitched, the tan looked beautiful.

![The tan Ghost Phantom tent set up at site 18 after sunset](tent-night.jpg)

With the tent finally up, the kids were hungry, so we started with the classic camping dream — meat grilled over a wood fire. It was exciting at first, but as it got darker, we couldn't even tell if the meat was cooked. I had dreamed of a leisurely camping trip, but between passing grilled meat to the kids nonstop, the day was more exhausting than relaxing.

![The family gathered around a campfire in front of the tent at night](campfire.jpg)

Round two was rosé tteokbokki that my daughter had made in her school cooking class. Then we were so tired we turned on the electric heating pad and all fell asleep. The April night air was still chilly, but thanks to the heating pad the kids slept warm — while the adults, sleeping on the outer edges, tossed and turned a bit.

![A frying pan full of bubbling rosé tteokbokki](rose-tteokbokki.jpg)

The next morning we looked around the campground. The restrooms, cooking area, and recycling station were all clean, and the hot water worked well.

![The covered outdoor cooking and dishwashing area](cooking-area.jpg)

![A vine tunnel walkway through the campground](tunnel-path.jpg)

We requested the checkout cart and went to the camp store early to buy trash bags, but even past opening time the store never opened. We ended up managing without bags, with some help from a staff member. That was a bit disappointing.

The kids played for a long time at the playground in front of the store, and there were photo spots everywhere, so we took plenty of pictures.

![Children playing at the large playground in the park](playground.jpg)

![A red circular flower-bed photo spot](photozone.jpg)

We also stopped by the **Osan Ecorium** next door. The exhibits were less impressive than expected, but there's free bicycle rental right in front. We wanted to ride... but we were too exhausted, so we just headed home.

![A child playing at the Ecorium's interactive media wall](ecorium.jpg)

Being only 30 minutes from home made this first trip feel low-pressure, and with so many things for kids to do nearby, it's a great campground for families. It was a first camp where we fumbled even driving a single stake — but first times are always exciting, aren't they?

**Malgeumteo Park Campground** — 52 Osancheon-ro, Osan-si, Gyeonggi-do
